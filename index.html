India Tax Residency & NRI/OCI Guide

How your citizenship, days in India, and Indian income determine whether your worldwide earnings become taxable in India

🇮🇳 Indian Citizens Abroad 🇺🇸 US Citizens / OCI Holders Covers Income Tax Act 1961 + Bill 2025
1 The Three Tax Residency Statuses

India taxes you based on your residency status — not your citizenship or passport. Status must be determined fresh each financial year (April 1 – March 31).

Status Who Qualifies What's Taxed in India What's NOT Taxed
NRI
Non-Resident		 Does not meet any residency condition for the FY Only income earned or received in India (Indian rent, capital gains on Indian assets, NRO interest, etc.) All overseas income — salary, investments, retirement accounts
RNOR
Resident but Not Ordinarily Resident		 Meets a residency condition BUT was NRI for 9 of last 10 years, or stayed ≤ 729 days in India in last 7 years Only Indian-sourced income (same as NRI) Overseas income remains exempt (unless from a business controlled in India)
ROR
Resident & Ordinarily Resident		 Meets residency condition AND fails both RNOR tests Worldwide income — everything globally Nothing is exempt (DTAA relief may apply)

4 The RNOR Safety Net

Even if you accidentally trigger "Resident" status, RNOR protects your overseas income from Indian taxation. You qualify for RNOR if you meet either condition below.

RNOR Condition What It Means Practical Check
9-out-of-10 Year Test You were NRI in at least 9 of the 10 financial years preceding the current FY If you've been in the US for 9+ years, you pass this. Even 1 year as resident in last 10 is okay.
729-Day Test Total physical presence in India ≤ 729 days during the 7 FYs preceding the current FY That's roughly 104 days/year average. If you visit 90 days/year for 7 years = 630 days — safe. At 110 days/year = 770 days — unsafe.
⚠️ RNOR is a safety net, not a strategy RNOR protects your overseas income, which is great. But it's temporary — once you've been visiting India frequently for many years, you'll eventually fail both tests and become full ROR. Use it as a backup, not your primary plan.

7 Real-World Scenarios

Covering both Indian citizens abroad and OCI holders in the USA.

Scenario A: OCI Holder, Comfortable Family Visits

US Citizen / OCI NRI — Safe
Days in India: 90 days/year for 5 years
Indian income: ₹5 lakh (NRO interest + small rental)
7-year cumulative: ~450 days (well under 729)
✅ Comfortably NRI. Under 182 days, income under ₹15L — no rule triggers. RNOR safety net intact. Only Indian income taxed.

Scenario B: OCI Holder, Extended 5-Month Stay

US Citizen / OCI NRI — Safe
Days in India: 150 days (long stay to care for aging parents)
Indian income: ₹8 lakh (rental income)
This is the year: One-time extended visit
✅ Still NRI! Since Indian income is under ₹15L, the 120-day rule doesn't trigger. You're under 182 days. The 60-day rule doesn't apply to OCI holders with income < ₹15L. Perfectly safe.

Scenario C: OCI Holder, Income Crosses ₹15 Lakh

US Citizen / OCI RNOR — Caution
Days in India: 130 days
Indian income: ₹18 lakh (rental + capital gains from property sale)
Past 4 years in India: 400 days (exceeds 365)
NRI history: NRI for 10 of last 10 years
⚠️ The 120-day rule triggers — you become a "Deemed Resident." But since you've been NRI for 9+/10 years, you qualify as RNOR. Your US salary, 401(k), and stock gains stay untaxed in India. Only the ₹18L Indian income is taxable.

Scenario D: Indian Citizen, Working in USA

Indian Citizen NRI — Safe
Days in India: 80 days/year
Indian income: ₹3 lakh (FD interest)
Left India for employment: Yes (H-1B → Green Card holder, Indian passport)
✅ NRI. The 60-day rule is exempt for Indian citizens who left for employment abroad. Under 182 days. No risk at all.

Scenario E: Indian Citizen, Not Taxed Anywhere (UAE)

Indian Citizen Deemed Resident
Days in India: 0 days
Indian income: ₹20 lakh (business income)
Resides in: UAE (no income tax)
⚠️ Deemed Resident! Even with ZERO days in India, the deemed residency rule makes this person a resident (RNOR status) because they're an Indian citizen with ₹15L+ Indian income and not taxed in any other country. Note: This rule would NOT apply if this person were a US citizen with OCI — it's for Indian citizens only.

Scenario F: OCI Holder, Gradual Overexposure

US Citizen / OCI ROR — Danger
Pattern: Year 1–4: 60 days → Year 5–8: 110 days → Year 9: 190 days
Indian income in Yr 9: ₹22 lakh (sold a property)
7-year cumulative (Yrs 2–8): 60×3 + 110×4 = 620 days — under 729 ✓
9/10 NRI test: NRI for 8 of 10 — fails! (was Resident in Yr 9, and was borderline another year)
❌ Year 9 is dangerous. 190 days makes you Resident. If you fail both RNOR tests (not NRI for 9/10 years AND over 729 cumulative days), you become full ROR — worldwide income including US salary, 401(k), dividends, everything becomes taxable in India. You'd need to rely on DTAA for relief.

Scenario G: Indian Citizen, Family Emergency Overstay

Indian Citizen ROR — Danger
Days in India this year: 200 days (parent's long illness)
Prior 7 years: Visited 115 days/year = 805 days (exceeds 729)
NRI in last 10 years: Only 7 out of 10 (fails 9/10 test)
❌ Full ROR. Worldwide income — US salary, retirement accounts, investment gains — all taxable in India. The 115 days/year seemed safe individually but compounded to destroy the 729-day safety net.

  • Count carefully: Both arrival and departure dates count as days in India

10 Double Taxation: India-USA DTAA

🤝 India-USA Double Taxation Avoidance Agreement

If you ever become ROR (worldwide income taxable in India), the DTAA between India and USA prevents you from being taxed twice on the same income. You can claim a Foreign Tax Credit in India for taxes already paid to the US (or vice versa) using Form 67. However, DTAA is a relief mechanism, not a prevention — you'd still need to file returns in both countries and the process is cumbersome. The smartest approach is to avoid becoming ROR in the first place.

📋 Practical Checklist ✓ Maintain a travel log: record every India entry and exit date, keep boarding passes and passport stamps
✓ Track your Indian income: rental, capital gains, NRO interest, FD — know when it approaches ₹15 lakh
✓ Check status each March: before the FY ends on March 31, count your days
✓ Plan trips around the April 1 boundary: a Dec–Mar trip and an Apr–Jun trip splits days across two FYs
✓ Consult a CA if selling Indian property: a single large capital gain can push you over the ₹15L threshold
✓ Review your 7-year cumulative annually: this protects your RNOR safety net
